Fungal Diseases

Common fungal infections affecting tomato plants

High Severity

Early Blight

Dark spots with concentric rings on leaves, stems, and fruits

Caused by the fungus Alternaria solani, early blight appears as small, dark spots that enlarge to form concentric rings. Lower leaves are affected first.

Management Strategies:

  • Remove and destroy infected plant debris
  • Apply copper-based fungicides
  • Ensure proper spacing for air circulation
  • Water at the base to keep foliage dry
  • Use resistant tomato varieties
High Severity

Late Blight

Water-soaked lesions on leaves that turn brown, white mold growth

Caused by Phytophthora infestans, late blight spreads rapidly in cool, wet conditions and can destroy entire crops within days.

Management Strategies:

  • Apply fungicides preventatively
  • Avoid overhead irrigation
  • Remove and destroy infected plants immediately
  • Use certified disease-free seeds
  • Practice crop rotation
Medium Severity

Powdery Mildew

White, powdery spots on leaves and stems

Caused by various fungal species, powdery mildew appears as white, talcum-powder-like spots that can cover leaves and reduce photosynthesis.

Management Strategies:

  • Apply sulfur-based fungicides
  • Improve air circulation around plants
  • Remove severely infected leaves
  • Avoid overhead watering
  • Use resistant varieties when available

Bacterial Diseases

Bacterial infections that affect tomato plants

Medium Severity

Bacterial Spot

Small, dark, scabby spots on fruits and leaves

Caused by Xanthomonas species, bacterial spot appears as small, water-soaked spots that become dark and scabby, affecting both leaves and fruits.

Management Strategies:

  • Use copper-based bactericides
  • Avoid working with plants when wet
  • Use disease-free seeds and transplants
  • Practice crop rotation
  • Remove and destroy infected plants
High Severity

Bacterial Wilt

Sudden wilting of plants without yellowing

Caused by Ralstonia solanacearum, bacterial wilt causes rapid wilting of the entire plant without leaf yellowing. The vascular system becomes brown.

Management Strategies:

  • Use resistant varieties
  • Practice long-term crop rotation
  • Solarize soil before planting
  • Avoid planting in infected soil
  • Remove and destroy infected plants

Viral Diseases

Viral infections transmitted by insects

Medium Severity

Tomato Mosaic Virus

Mottled light and dark green areas on leaves

This virus causes mosaic patterns on leaves, leaf distortion, and stunted growth. It can be spread through contaminated tools, hands, or insects.

Management Strategies:

  • Use virus-resistant varieties
  • Control aphid populations
  • Disinfect tools between plants
  • Remove and destroy infected plants
  • Wash hands after handling infected plants
High Severity

Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl

Upward curling of leaves, yellowing, stunted growth

Transmitted by whiteflies, this virus causes severe stunting, leaf curling, and yellowing. Fruits are small and poorly developed.

Management Strategies:

  • Control whitefly populations
  • Use resistant varieties
  • Remove infected plants immediately
  • Use reflective mulches to deter whiteflies
  • Practice good weed control

Disease Prevention

Proactive measures to keep your tomato plants healthy

Essential Prevention Practices

Use Resistant Varieties

Select tomato varieties with natural resistance to common diseases in your area.

Crop Rotation

Rotate tomatoes with non-solanaceous crops to break disease cycles.

Proper Watering

Water at the base of plants to keep foliage dry and prevent fungal growth.

Good Air Circulation

Space plants properly and prune to improve airflow and reduce humidity.

Pest Control

Manage insect populations that can spread viral diseases.

Sanitation

Clean tools, remove plant debris, and disinfect between seasons.
